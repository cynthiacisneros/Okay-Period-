What Is a Period?

Take this one gentle step at a time. Open a section when you’re ready—there’s no rush, no quiz at the end, just plain-language reassurance about what periods are and how they can show up.

A period is a few days each month when a little blood and tissue leave your body through your vagina.

For many people with a uterus, it’s a normal part of life—your body refreshing the lining it built up inside.

About once a month, your body prepares for the possibility of pregnancy. If that doesn’t happen, the extra lining isn’t needed anymore.

It sheds and flows out—that’s your period. The whole rhythm is called the menstrual cycle.

✿

Your body is doing something natural. Everyone’s experience can look a little different—and that’s okay.

Hormones gently rise and fall on a loop—like a quiet monthly beat. That pattern helps your uterus stay healthy.

Having a period usually means things are working as they’re meant to, not that something is “wrong” with you.

Some people barely notice a period. Others feel cramps, tiredness, sore muscles, or big emotions before or during bleeding.

That doesn’t make you dramatic—it’s common. Rest, water, and being kind to yourself can help.

Many people get a first period between ages 9 and 16—sometimes around when a parent or older sibling started, sometimes not.

Starting earlier or later doesn’t define you. A trusted adult or clinician can help if you’re unsure what’s normal for you.

◇

You are not weird, and you are not alone. Wondering about your body is care—not something to hide.

Flow, timing, and how you feel can shift month to month. The first few years are often especially uneven—and that’s typical for many people.

If something hurts a lot, scares you, or you’re soaking through protection very fast, reach out to a healthcare provider. You deserve support.
