What Is a Period?

Scroll slowly if you like. Each question below is a stopping place—the answers appear as you reach them, so you never have to take in everything at once.

What’s Actually Happening?

About once a month, your body prepares for the possibility of pregnancy. If that doesn’t happen, the extra lining inside your uterus isn’t needed anymore.

It sheds and flows out through your vagina—that bleeding is your period. The whole rhythm is called the menstrual cycle.

Why Do Periods Happen?

Hormones rise and fall in a quiet monthly loop. That pattern keeps your uterus healthy and in step with the rest of you.

For many people, having a period means things are working as they’re meant to—not that something is wrong.

Your body is doing something natural. Everyone’s experience can look a little different—and that’s okay.

What Might I Feel?

Some people barely notice a period. Others feel cramps, tiredness, sore muscles, or big emotions before or during bleeding.

That doesn’t make you dramatic—it’s common. Rest, water, and a little patience with yourself go a long way.

When Do Periods Start?

A first period often shows up between ages 9 and 16—sometimes around when a parent or older sibling started, sometimes not.

Starting earlier or later doesn’t define you. A trusted adult or clinician can help if you’re unsure what’s normal for you.

You are not weird, and you are not alone. Being curious about your body is a kind of care.

Is This Normal?

Flow, timing, and how you feel can shift from month to month. The first few years are often especially uneven—and that’s typical for lots of people.

If something hurts a lot, scares you, or you’re soaking through protection very quickly, reach out to a healthcare provider. You deserve support, not silence.
